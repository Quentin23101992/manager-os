Opening Procedure

Everything that needs to happen before the first customer walks in. Sequential — don't skip steps.

·

All Types Arrival & Security (T-90 min)

  • Disarm alarm system, record entry time
  • Walk full perimeter — check locks, windows, signs of break-in
  • Turn on lights, HVAC, music system
  • Check voicemail / emails for cancellations, delivery changes, staff call-outs
  • Review the day's reservations / expected covers

Critical Kitchen & Food Safety (T-75 min)

  • Check all fridge/freezer temperatures — log readings (fridges: 0-4°C, freezers: -18°C or below)
  • Inspect yesterday's prep — discard anything past use-by date
  • Verify delivery arrived correctly (cross-check delivery note vs. order)
  • Check all equipment is working: ovens, fryers, grills, dishwasher
  • Ensure handwash stations stocked: soap, paper towels, sanitizer
  • Brief kitchen team on specials, 86'd items, expected volume

All Types Front of House (T-45 min)

  • Tables set / wiped down, chairs in position
  • Check bathrooms — clean, stocked, functional
  • POS system on, float counted and verified (record amount)
  • Menus clean and current (prices, specials, allergen info)
  • Check floor for hazards — wet spots, loose cables, broken fixtures

All Types Team Briefing (T-15 min)

  • Confirm who's on shift, assign stations/sections
  • Communicate specials, promos, 86'd items
  • Flag VIPs, large parties, allergies on reservations
  • Set one focus for the shift (e.g., "upsell desserts" or "table turn time")
  • Ask: "Anyone have questions or issues before we open?"

Pub/Casual Kitchen-Specific Opening (Frog Model)

  • Review handover notes from previous shift — preps completed, still needed, DLC alerts, stock issues, equipment
  • Control ALL DLCs — walk-in fridges + base fridges. Pull anything expired.
  • Verify fridge/freezer temps → record on Trail (not from memory — actual reading)
  • Check cleaning checklist was signed from previous shift
  • Check oil quality (visual + smell) — record on daily sheet
  • Confirm team roster + assign sections (Pass, Burger, Salad, Fryer, Plonge)
  • Write prep plan per cook: item, amount, start time, estimated duration
  • Check last week's stock audit for ordering needs
  • Review bookings / events (sports matches, private parties) — adjust prep volume
✅
Manager Mindset: The opening sets the tone. If you're rushed and scattered, the team feels it. Arrive early enough that you're calm by the time staff show up. Your energy is contagious — in both directions.

Closing Procedure

End-of-day lockdown. The goal: tomorrow's opener walks into a clean, prepped, secure restaurant.

·

All Types Kitchen Close-Down

  • All food stored, labeled (item + date), covered
  • Fridge/freezer temps logged (final reading)
  • All cooking surfaces cleaned, grills scraped, fryers filtered or off
  • Floors swept and mopped, drains clear
  • Trash taken out, bins relined
  • Equipment turned off (except fridges/freezers)
  • Prep list written for tomorrow

All Types Front of House Close

  • Last customers served, tabs closed
  • Tables cleared, wiped, reset for tomorrow
  • Bathrooms checked and cleaned
  • Floor swept/vacuumed
  • Condiments refilled, menus wiped

Critical Cash & Admin

  • Run end-of-day POS report (Z-report)
  • Count till — compare to POS total, note variance
  • Prepare bank deposit if required
  • Record daily sales total, covers, average ticket
  • Log any incidents, complaints, staff issues
  • Review tomorrow's reservations and staffing

Pub/Casual Kitchen Handover Checklist (Frog Model)

  • All daily cleaning tasks checked AND signed (not just done — documented)
  • Oil temperature recorded on daily sheet
  • Bins taken out — both kitchen AND salle
  • Prep list for next shift completed and checked
  • Plonge empty and clean
  • Floor swept and mopped
  • All surfaces wiped down
  • Handover notes written: preps done, preps still needed, DLC alerts, stock issues, equipment problems, anything the next shift needs to know

All Types Security Close

  • All doors and windows locked
  • Gas turned off (if applicable)
  • Lights off (except security lights)
  • Alarm system armed
  • Final walkthrough — nobody left in building

Cold Chain Verification (non-negotiable)

  • Fridge/freezer closing temps logged on Trail
  • Hot food cooled properly (<90min to fridge temp) and stored
  • All preps labelled with DLC sticker
  • Walk-in final check: DLC/FIFO order, no cardboard, no bins inside

Records & Handover Completion

  • Waste logged (weights + reasons)
  • Trail app entries completed for the shift
  • Cleaning checklist signed off by closing manager
  • Floor swept and mopped (kitchen + service areas)
  • Bins emptied, lids on, taken to designated area
  • Handover notes written for next shift opener

Hygiene & HACCP

Food safety is non-negotiable. HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) is the system that prevents foodborne illness. As manager, you're legally responsible.

Critical Temperature Control — The #1 Rule

The "danger zone" is 5°C to 63°C (EU standard). French cold storage targets 0-4°C as extra safety margin. Bacteria multiply rapidly in this range — especially 30-40°C. Your job: keep food out of it.

StorageTargetAction if exceeded
Fridge0–4°CIf above 8°C: check immediately, move items, call technician
Freezer-18°C or belowIf above -15°C: do not refreeze thawed items, escalate
Hot holding63°C or aboveFood below 63°C for >2hrs must be discarded
Cooking (poultry)Core temp 75°CUse probe thermometer to verify
Cooling cooked food63°C → 10°C within 2 hoursThen store at 0-3°C. Use blast chiller, ice baths, shallow containers

Critical Allergen Management

In France/EU: 14 major allergens must be declared. As manager you must ensure:

  • Allergen information is available for every menu item (written, not just verbal)
  • Kitchen staff know which dishes contain which allergens
  • Cross-contamination prevention: separate utensils, surfaces, storage
  • When a customer declares an allergy: manager verifies with kitchen before food goes out
⚠️
Legal liability: An allergen incident can result in criminal prosecution of the manager. This is not optional compliance — it's personal legal exposure.

All Types Daily Hygiene Checks

  • Staff: clean uniforms, hair tied back, no jewelry, wounds covered with blue plasters
  • Handwashing observed and stations stocked
  • Fridge temps logged (morning, midday, evening)
  • Use-by dates checked on all open items
  • Cleaning schedule followed (signed off)
  • No food stored on floor — minimum 15cm above ground
  • Raw/cooked separated in storage (raw below cooked)
  • Pest control signs checked (droppings, damage, sightings)

Real Talk: Why Kitchens Fail Audits

Based on 6+ actual audit reports from a multi-site operation:

  • #1 failure: Documentation gaps. "Auto-controls" (delivery records, fridge temps, cleaning logs) is consistently the weakest category. Staff DO the work but DON'T record it — or record fake numbers (all deliveries logged as 4°C exactly = nobody's actually measuring).
  • #2 failure: DLC compliance. Missing stickers, expired products still in walk-in, sauces kept past their date in prep containers instead of proper bacs. This one yo-yos: improves after an audit, slides back within weeks.
  • #3 failure: Fridge cleanliness. The inside of service fridges is the most persistent hygiene failure across venues. It's dirty, it stays dirty, it gets called out every audit.
  • #4 failure: Follow-through. Action plans get written after bad audits but never fully completed. The highest-value item in the simplified audit (3/50 points) is literally "was the previous action plan completed?" — because it almost never is.
✅
Manager takeaway: Your job isn't to catch every hygiene issue yourself. It's to build a system where issues get caught AND fixed AND stay fixed. That means: daily checks (not weekly), signed documentation (not verbal), and follow-up on your own action plans.

HACCP Documentation — What You Must Keep

Inspectors will ask for these. Keep them organized and accessible:

  • Temperature logs — fridge, freezer, hot holding, delivery temps
  • Cleaning schedules — signed and dated
  • Delivery records — supplier, date, temp on arrival, condition
  • Staff training records — who completed food safety training, when
  • Allergen matrix — every dish mapped to its allergens
  • Incident log — any food safety incidents, complaints, corrective actions

Rush Management

The lunch/dinner rush is where managers earn their salary. Your job isn't to do everyone's work — it's to keep the system flowing.

The Manager's Role During Rush

You are the system regulator, not an extra pair of hands. If you're stuck making plates, nobody's watching the whole floor.

  • Position yourself at the pass (between kitchen and floor) — this is the control point
  • Monitor ticket times — if a table waits 20+ minutes without food, intervene
  • Read the room — spot tables that need attention before staff do
  • Manage the door — control seating pace to match kitchen capacity
  • Jump in surgically — run food, clear tables, but return to oversight fast

Rush Escalation Ladder

SignalWhat It MeansYour Action
Tickets stackingKitchen falling behindCheck if someone's in the weeds. Redistribute or jump in for one task.
Tables waiting 10+ min for greetingFOH overwhelmedGreet tables yourself, or reassign sections temporarily.
Wrong dishes going outCommunication breakdownSlow down the pass. Call out every ticket clearly before it leaves.
Staff snapping at each otherStress boiling overBrief calm word: "I see you, we've got this." Resolve after rush.
Customer visibly upsetService failureGo to table immediately. Acknowledge, apologize, fix.
✅
AuDHD Tip (F2): Rush can trigger hyperfocus (good) or sensory overload (bad). Pre-plan your position and your scan pattern (kitchen → floor → door → repeat). Having a physical loop prevents freeze-up when everything's loud and fast. If you feel the overload building: one deep breath, refocus on the NEXT ticket only. Your nervous system works in sequences, not multitasking (Operator's Manual: ns-01).
🔍
F1 (Race): During rush, when you're directing people urgently, a Black man giving rapid commands can be read differently than a white manager doing the same. This is the code-switching tax (Operator's Manual: nav-02). Practical counter: establish your authority in calm moments (pre-shift briefing, station assignments) so that during rush, people already know who's in charge. The briefing is not just operational — it's social positioning.
F3 (Gender): Female staff in the weeds may receive less help from male colleagues than male staff in the same position. Monitor and redistribute support evenly.

Cash & Till Management

Cash handling errors are the fastest way to lose trust — or your job. Be methodical.

Critical Daily Cash Procedure

  • Opening float: Count it yourself, record the amount, sign off. Never trust "it was fine yesterday."
  • During service: Only designated staff handle the till. Manager approval for voids/discounts/refunds.
  • Closing: Run Z-report → count physical cash → compare to POS total.
Variance = Physical Cash − Expected Cash (from POS)

Acceptable variance: ±€5. Anything beyond that needs investigation and documentation.

Red Flags to Watch

  • Frequent voids by the same staff member — could indicate theft or training gap
  • Cash always short on the same shift — pattern points to a person or process
  • No-sale openings — till opened without a transaction (needs explanation)
  • Discounts without manager approval — tighten the approval chain
⚠️
Never accuse — investigate. Check camera footage + POS logs before having a conversation. "I noticed a variance" is better than "you're stealing."
🔍
F1 (Race): Cash discrepancies + Black manager = heightened suspicion from ownership. Protect yourself: always count with a witness or on camera. Document everything. Your paper trail is your shield. This isn't paranoia — it's the operational reality of being a Black person responsible for money in a system with documented racial bias.

Incident Protocols

When things go wrong, your response speed and clarity define you as a manager.

Critical Customer Complaint — The LAST Framework

  • Listen — Let them talk. Don't interrupt. Show you're hearing them.
  • Apologize — "I'm sorry this happened" (not "I'm sorry you feel that way").
  • Solve — Offer a concrete fix: remake the dish, remove from bill, offer a free item.
  • Thank — "Thank you for telling me, it helps us improve."
✅
Most people don't want free stuff — they want to be heard. A genuine apology + quick fix resolves 90% of complaints.

Staff Injury

  • Administer first aid immediately (burns: cold water 10+ min)
  • If serious: call emergency services, do not move the person
  • Record in accident book: what happened, when, witnesses
  • Report to owner/area manager same day
  • Review: could this have been prevented? Fix the root cause.

Foodborne Illness Report

  • Take the complaint seriously — record every detail (what they ate, when, symptoms)
  • Isolate the suspected batch immediately
  • Notify owner/management — this may require contacting health authorities
  • Do NOT destroy evidence (food samples, packaging)
  • Review HACCP records for the day in question

Aggressive Customer / Security

  • Stay calm. Lower your voice (it forces them to lower theirs).
  • Do not engage physically. De-escalate or create distance.
  • If threat is real: call police, protect staff and other customers first.
  • Document everything after the fact.
🔍
Triple Filter — Incidents:
F1 (Race): When police are called, a Black manager on scene changes the dynamic. You may be treated as the problem, not the person who called. Strategy: when calling police, immediately identify yourself as the manager. Have your name badge visible. If possible, have a staff member (ideally white, if we're being honest about how this works) meet the officers at the door and point them to you. Documentation is your shield: incident log, timestamps, witness names.
F2 (AuDHD): Incidents are sensory bombs — shouting, adrenaline, multiple inputs at once. Your freeze response may activate before your problem-solving kicks in. The LAST framework (Listen, Apologize, Solve, Thank) works precisely because it's sequential — follow the steps, don't try to process the whole situation at once. Having it memorized means you don't need executive function to start responding.
F3 (Gender): Female staff may face more aggressive or sexualized behavior from problem customers. When a female team member reports aggression, take it at face value — don't minimize or suggest she "handle it differently." Your intervention as manager protects her and signals to the whole team that this behavior isn't tolerated.

Scheduling

A good schedule balances coverage, labor cost, fairness, and legal compliance. A bad one breaks teams.

Scheduling Principles

  • Staff to demand, not to habit. Check historical sales data for each shift — don't just copy last week.
  • Post schedules 2 weeks out minimum. Late schedules destroy morale and prevent people from planning their lives.
  • Track labor % in real time. Target: 25-35% of revenue. Adjust mid-week if sales are trending up or down.
  • Cross-train everyone. If only one person can do a critical role, you're one sick day from chaos.
  • Respect legal requirements. In France (HCR convention): 11h minimum rest between shifts, 39h/week is the effective standard (35h base + 4h at +10%), 2 rest days/week (can be non-consecutive), max 48h/week absolute.

Dealing with Call-Outs

  • Have a call list — ranked by availability and role capability
  • Ask, don't guilt. "Can you cover today?" not "You have to come in"
  • Track patterns. Frequent same-day call-outs on the same day of week = a conversation needed
  • Worst case: You cover the gap yourself. This is why you should be trained on every station.
🔍
Triple Filter Notes:
F1 (Race): As a Black manager in France, your scheduling decisions may face more pushback than a white manager's identical decisions. Research on racial discrimination in French restaurant hiring (TEPP 2025, INSEE studies) shows systemic bias. Strategy: document your reasoning. "I scheduled based on projected covers" is harder to challenge than a verbal decision.
F2 (AuDHD): Scheduling is a high-executive-function task — multiple variables, fairness tracking, future projection. Do it at your peak energy window, not end of shift when you're depleted. Use a template that reduces decisions to fill-in-the-blank.
F3 (Gender): Female staff may face pressure to accommodate others' schedules more than male staff. Monitor for this pattern in swap requests.
💡
France-specific: Code du travail imposes strict scheduling rules. Changing a posted schedule less than 7 days out requires employee agreement. Dimanche travaillé (Sunday work) has specific compensation rules. Know your convention collective (sector-specific labor agreement).

Delegation

If you're doing everything yourself, you're not managing — you're just the hardest-working employee. Delegation is the core management skill.

The Delegation Framework

  • What — Be specific: "I need you to set up tables 12-18 with full settings" not "handle the floor"
  • Why — Give context: "We have a group of 30 at 8pm" → people work better when they understand the reason
  • When — Set a deadline: "Done by 7:30" not "when you can"
  • Check — Verify it was done to standard. Not micromanaging — quality control.

What to Delegate vs. Keep

✅
AuDHD Tip: Delegation is hard when you hyperfocus on doing things "right." The standard doesn't have to be YOUR standard — it has to be the RESTAURANT'S standard. If it's 80% as good as you'd do it, that's a win. Perfect is the enemy of done.
🔍
Triple Filter — Delegation:
F1 (Race): When a Black manager delegates, the response isn't always the same as when a white manager delegates. Research on racial authority bias shows that directives from Black leaders face more pushback and are more likely to be "forgotten." Counter: delegate with specifics (What, Why, When, Check) so the task is unambiguous. Vague delegation leaves room for selective compliance.
F2 (AuDHD): Your pattern-recognition strength means you often see the fastest path to completion — and get frustrated when others don't. But explaining your reasoning ("I need this done this way because...") isn't micromanaging, it's translating your pattern recognition into instructions NT brains can follow.
F3 (Gender): Women who delegate firmly are rated as "bossy" or "aggressive" more than men doing the same. If you manage female staff, watch whether they face this dynamic from the rest of the team when they take charge of delegated tasks.

Conflict Resolution

Conflict is normal in high-pressure kitchens. Your job: contain it, resolve it, prevent it from becoming culture.

During Service — Contain Mode

Never resolve conflicts during rush. The protocol:

  • Separate the people: "Hey, I need you on station X for now."
  • Brief, firm: "We'll sort this out after service. Right now, we serve."
  • Note what happened so you don't forget (phone note, paper).

After Service — Resolve Mode

  • Hear both sides separately first. Never triangulate in the moment.
  • Focus on behavior, not character: "You raised your voice at Alex during service" not "You're disrespectful."
  • Find the root: Most conflicts are about workload, fairness, or communication breakdowns — not personality.
  • Agree on next steps together: "What would make this work better for both of you?"
  • Document if it's serious. If this could escalate, you need a paper trail.
⚠️
Never tolerate: harassment, discrimination, or threats. These are immediate escalation to owner/HR, not mediation material. Know the line between conflict and misconduct.
🔍
Triple Filter Notes:
F1 (Race): As a Black manager mediating conflict, you face a specific trap: if you show emotion, you risk being labeled "angry" or "aggressive" — a stereotype that doesn't apply to white managers showing the same intensity. Strategy: stay factual, stay calm, but don't over-correct into emotional suppression. That's masking cost you don't need to pay (Operator's Manual: nav-02, code-switching). The goal is conscious control, not anxious performance.
F2 (AuDHD): Conflict is sensory + emotional overload. Your nervous system may go into fight/flight faster than you realize (Operator's Manual: ns-01, reg-01). The "contain during service / resolve after" protocol isn't just good management — it's self-regulation. You need the time gap to process. Use it.
F3 (Gender): Watch for gendered patterns: female staff raising the same concerns as male staff being dismissed as "emotional" or "dramatic." Your job as manager is to hear the content, not evaluate the delivery through gender bias.

Performance Management

Good performance management isn't annual reviews — it's daily micro-feedback that keeps people growing.

Daily Feedback (The 80%)

Most performance management happens in 10-second moments:

  • Catch people doing things right: "Great upsell on table 7" — specific praise builds repetition
  • Correct in private, praise in public. Pull someone aside: "Hey, quick note on how you plated that..."
  • Be immediate. Feedback 3 days later is useless. Do it in the moment or right after service.

The Difficult Conversation Framework

For performance issues that need a formal sit-down:

  • Facts first: "You were 15 minutes late 3 times this week" — not "you're unreliable"
  • Impact: "That means your section wasn't set, and your teammates had to cover"
  • Their perspective: "What's going on?" — there might be context you don't know
  • Clear expectation: "I need you on time for every shift. Can we agree on that?"
  • Support offer: "Is there anything that would help? Different shift time?"
  • Consequence: "If it continues, we'll need to go formal" — state it clearly, not as a threat
🔍
Triple Filter Notes:
F1 (Race): Performance evaluation is where unconscious bias hits hardest. Studies show identical behavior is rated differently by race. As a Black manager, your credibility in giving feedback may be questioned — especially when correcting white staff. Counter: always tie feedback to observable behavior + measurable impact. "Table 7 waited 12 minutes" is unchallengeable. "You seemed unfocused" is subjective and vulnerable to pushback.
F2 (AuDHD): "Be immediate" with feedback is great advice, but your processing speed may need a beat. It's OK to say "quick word after this table is served" to give yourself 60 seconds to organize your thoughts. The feedback is still timely. Blurting out half-formed corrections is worse than a brief, structured one.
F3 (Gender): Performance research shows women receive more personality-based feedback ("be more confident") while men get skill-based feedback ("improve your plating speed"). Give everyone behavior-based, skill-based feedback.

Onboarding New Staff

First impressions set the trajectory. A structured first week prevents months of confusion.

Day 1 Checklist

  • Uniform sorted, locker assigned, staff meal explained
  • Tour of premises: kitchen, storage, bathrooms, fire exits, staff area
  • Introduce to every team member by name and role
  • Food safety basics: handwashing, allergens, temp control
  • Shadow an experienced staff member for full shift
  • Show them the menu — have them taste key dishes if possible
  • Explain scheduling, breaks, communication channels
  • End of day: "How was it? What questions do you have?"

Week 1 Milestones

  • Day 1-2: Shadow only. Learn layout, names, rhythm.

What to Focus On

As a manager, you directly control 2 of the 3 biggest cost lines:

  • Food cost (COGS): 28-35% is typical (2024 NRA data). You control this through portioning, ordering, waste, and menu engineering.
  • Labor cost: 25-35% depending on restaurant type. You control this through scheduling.
  • Revenue: You influence this through table turns, upselling training, and not losing customers.
Prime Cost = Food Cost % + Labor Cost %

If prime cost exceeds 65%, the restaurant is probably losing money — even if it looks busy.

Food Cost Management

Food cost is the percentage of revenue spent on ingredients. It's the number that separates profitable restaurants from failing ones.

The Formula

Food Cost % = (Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Food Revenue) × 100

Example: If you sold €10,000 in food and used €3,000 in ingredients → 30% food cost.

TypeTypical Target
QSR / Fast food28-32%
Casual dining30-34%
Fine dining32-35%

The 5 Levers You Control

  • Portioning: Standardized recipes with exact weights. "A handful" is not a portion.
  • Ordering: Order to projected sales, not to fill the shelves. Over-ordering = waste.
  • Receiving: Check every delivery: quantity, quality, temperature. Reject what doesn't meet standards.
  • Storage: FIFO (First In First Out). Rotate stock religiously.
  • Waste tracking: If you don't measure it, you can't manage it. Log every waste event.

Labor Cost Management

Labor is usually the biggest or second-biggest expense. Scheduling is the main tool you have to control it.

The Formula

Labor Cost % = (Total Labor Costs ÷ Total Revenue) × 100

Total labor = wages + social charges + overtime + benefits. In France, charges patronales add 25-42% on top of gross salary (varies by wage level). Near SMIC: as low as 5-10% thanks to RGDU exoneration (2026 reform). Higher salaries: up to 40%+.

TypeTypical Target
QSR / Fast food28-32%
Casual dining / Full-service33-37%
Fine dining35-40%

How to Control Labor Cost

  • Schedule to sales forecast: Busy Friday = full team. Dead Tuesday = skeleton crew. Don't schedule from habit.
  • Stagger start times: Not everyone needs to arrive at the same time. Prep cook at 9, line cook at 10, FOH at 11.
  • Cut early when it's dead: "Hey, it's quiet — want to head home early?" Most staff appreciate it. Track who you send home to keep it fair.
  • Minimize overtime: In HCR convention: 36-39h = +10%, 40-43h = +20%, 44h+ = +50%. Lower than standard rates but still adds up. Contingent: 360h/year max. Track weekly hours actively.
  • Cross-training: A server who can bartend, a line cook who can prep = flexible scheduling with fewer total hours.

Ordering & Inventory

Ordering is where food cost is won or lost. Order too much = waste. Order too little = 86'd items and lost revenue.

The Ordering Process

  • Count current stock (par check)
  • Check upcoming covers (reservations + historical data for that day)
  • Calculate need: Projected usage − current stock = order quantity
  • Compare to par levels (minimum stock you should always have)
  • Place order with enough lead time (know each supplier's delivery schedule)
Order Quantity = Par Level − Current Stock + Safety Buffer

Inventory Best Practices

  • FIFO: First In, First Out. New deliveries go BEHIND existing stock.
  • Weekly full count: Same day each week, same person, same method. Consistency matters more than precision.
  • Date everything: If it's not labeled, it doesn't exist (treat as expired).
  • Lock high-value items: Expensive proteins, alcohol — controlled access.
  • Track variance: Theoretical usage (recipes × dishes sold) vs. actual usage (inventory count). Gap = waste, theft, or portioning issues.

Every item in the bin is money you already spent. You can't eliminate waste, but you can minimize and understand it.

Break-Even & Margins

Break-even is the point where revenue covers all costs. Below it, you're losing money. Above it, you're profitable.

Break-Even Calculation

Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs ÷ (1 − Variable Cost %)

Example: Rent + utilities + insurance = €15,000/month. Variable costs (food + labor) = 60% of revenue.

€15,000 ÷ (1 − 0.60) = €15,000 ÷ 0.40 = €37,500/month needed to break even

That means: €37,500 in sales before you make €1 of profit. Everything above that is your margin.

Key Margin Concepts

  • Gross margin: Revenue − food cost. Shows how much you keep after ingredients. Target: 65-72%.
  • Contribution margin: Revenue − all variable costs. What each sale contributes to covering fixed costs.
  • Net margin: What's left after everything. Restaurants typically: 3-10%. Yes, it's thin.
💡
Restaurants are high-revenue, low-margin businesses. A 5% net margin on €50,000/month revenue = €2,500 profit. A 2% food cost improvement on that same revenue = €1,000 saved. Small percentages, big money.

"Why restaurant management?"

What they're looking for: Genuine motivation (they've heard "I love food" 1000 times).

Personalize this. What's real for you: The problem-solving, the tangible results, the team aspect, the pace. Be specific about what draws you to it. Avoid generic passion statements.

🐸 Your Secret Weapon: Real Operational Knowledge

You have something most candidates don't: 6+ years inside a real multi-site operation. Use it.

  • When asked about hygiene: "I've seen audit scores drop from 87 to 62 in the same venue because compliance slides without persistent oversight. The documents exist — DLC tables, cleaning checklists, audit templates. What's missing is someone making sure they're followed consistently across shifts."
  • When asked about P&L: "I understand how daily P&L tracking works in practice — AM/PM splits, hourly milestones against staff cost, beer/wet/food breakdowns. I know that 30% staff cost of HT sales is the benchmark and that going above it erodes profit fast."
  • When asked about team: "I've worked shifts where the KSM plan had section assignments, individual prep tasks with times, and a 16h-readiness checklist. I know what a structured shift looks like — and I know what happens when it's not there."
  • When asked about food safety failures: "The most common recurring failures I've seen are DLC compliance, fridge cleanliness, and documentation gaps — the same issues showing up audit after audit. The fix isn't one big cleanup, it's daily monitoring and follow-through on action plans."
